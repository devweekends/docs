Relocating internationally is one of the highest-impact career moves a software engineer can make, but it requires careful evaluation beyond just salary numbers. A role paying 30% more in a country with 50% higher cost of living is actually a step backward financially. This page provides a structured framework for thinking through relocation decisions.

## Key Evaluation Factors

When comparing countries, assess each factor relative to your current situation:

- **Visa pathways and timelines** -- Some countries offer fast-track tech visas (Germany’s Blue Card, Canada’s Global Talent Stream, the Netherlands’ Highly Skilled Migrant visa). Others require employer sponsorship that ties you to a single company for years. Know the difference -- it affects your negotiating power and long-term flexibility.
- **Salary vs. cost of living** -- Use tools like Numbeo, Expatistan, or Levels.fyi (filtered by country) to compare real purchasing power. A $120K salary in San Francisco and a $70K salary in Berlin can offer similar lifestyles once you account for housing, taxes, and healthcare.
- **Tech hub density and employer options** -- Moving somewhere with only one or two major tech employers is risky. If that job does not work out, you may need to relocate again. Prefer cities with a deep bench of employers: Berlin, Amsterdam, London, Toronto, Singapore, Dubai.
- **Language and cultural integration** -- English-friendly workplaces are common in tech, but daily life outside the office varies. Countries like the Netherlands, Germany, and the Nordic countries have high English proficiency. Others may require learning the local language for banking, housing, and social integration.
- **Relocation assistance and settling-in support** -- Some companies offer comprehensive relocation packages (flights, temporary housing, visa sponsorship, tax advisory). Others offer a flat cash amount. Factor in the hidden costs: first/last month rent deposits, furniture, local SIM cards, and the productivity dip during your first 2-3 months.
- **Tax structure** -- Some countries have favorable tax regimes for skilled migrants (the Netherlands’ 30% ruling, Portugal’s NHR regime). These can make a significant financial difference in your first years and should be factored into any salary comparison.
- **Quality of life and long-term residency path** -- Consider healthcare access, safety, public transportation, and the path to permanent residency or citizenship if you intend to stay long-term.

## Research Strategy

1. Start by identifying 3-5 target countries based on your priorities (salary, lifestyle, visa accessibility).
2. For each country, find at least 5 companies with active engineering teams using LinkedIn and job boards.
3. Connect with engineers already working in those countries -- Pakistani, Indian, and other South Asian tech communities abroad are often very willing to share advice.
4. Calculate your net take-home pay after taxes and cost of living adjustments, not just the gross salary figure.

<Tip>
Do not make relocation decisions based solely on salary. The engineers who are happiest abroad planned for integration, not just income. Talk to at least 3 people already living where you want to go before committing.
</Tip>
