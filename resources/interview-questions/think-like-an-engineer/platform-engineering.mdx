# Platform Engineering & DevOps -- Infrastructure, Automation, and Developer Experience

*Platform engineering is the discipline of building the foundation that every other engineer ships on. A good platform team is invisible -- developers deploy without thinking about infrastructure, secrets rotate without anyone noticing, and incidents resolve before users feel them. A bad platform team is a bottleneck -- every deployment requires a ticket, every environment is a snowflake, and "works on my machine" is the most commonly heard phrase in standup. This chapter covers how senior platform engineers think about Kubernetes, infrastructure as code, CI/CD, observability, and the increasingly important discipline of developer experience.*

---

## Real-World Stories: Why This Matters

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="How Spotify Scaled to 2,000 Microservices with Backstage">

By 2019, Spotify had over 2,000 microservices, 350+ engineering teams, and a growing crisis: engineers spent more time searching for documentation, configuring infrastructure, and figuring out who owned what than they spent writing code. Onboarding a new engineer took weeks because they had to navigate a maze of Confluence pages, Slack channels, and tribal knowledge to understand how to deploy a service.

Spotify's answer was **Backstage** -- an open-source internal developer portal that became the single front door for all engineering activities. Backstage provides: a software catalog (every service, its owner, dependencies, documentation, and health status), templates for creating new services (with pre-configured CI/CD, monitoring, and access controls), and a plugin architecture that integrates every internal tool (PagerDuty, Kubernetes, Datadog, Jenkins).

The impact was transformative: time to create a new production service dropped from 2 weeks to 15 minutes. Developer satisfaction scores increased by 40%. Onboarding time dropped by 55%. And critically, the platform team stopped being the bottleneck -- developers could self-serve infrastructure through Backstage without filing tickets or waiting for approvals.

Spotify donated Backstage to the Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF), and by 2025 it has become the de facto standard for internal developer portals, adopted by companies including Netflix, Expedia, HP, and the US Department of Defense.

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="How Google's Borg Became Kubernetes -- and Changed Everything">

Google's internal cluster management system, Borg, had been orchestrating containers since 2003 -- a decade before Docker existed. Every Google service, from Search to Gmail to YouTube, ran on Borg. The system managed billions of containers across millions of machines.

In 2014, Google open-sourced a distillation of Borg's ideas as **Kubernetes**. The bet was strategic: if the industry adopted Kubernetes as the standard container orchestrator, Google Cloud Platform (GCP) would have a natural advantage since Kubernetes was designed by the engineers who built and operated the system it was based on.

The bet paid off beyond Google's expectations. Kubernetes became the universal abstraction layer for container orchestration, and it changed how the entire industry thinks about infrastructure. Instead of managing servers, teams manage desired state: "I want 3 replicas of this container, with 2 CPU and 4GB memory, accessible on port 8080." Kubernetes handles scheduling, scaling, self-healing, and networking.

But Kubernetes also introduced enormous complexity. Companies that adopted it without the operational maturity to manage it spent more time debugging Kubernetes than debugging their applications. The lesson that Google learned internally (and that many companies are still learning): **the power of Kubernetes comes from the platform layer built on top of it, not from Kubernetes itself.** Google's internal developers never interact with Borg directly -- they interact with higher-level abstractions. The companies that succeed with Kubernetes are those that build similar abstractions for their developers.

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="How Netflix Built a Zero-Downtime Deployment Machine">

Netflix deploys thousands of times per day across hundreds of microservices, and they do it while serving 250+ million subscribers. Their deployment philosophy: **every deploy is a canary.**

Netflix's deployment pipeline (Spinnaker, which they open-sourced) works like this: when a developer merges to main, a CI pipeline builds and tests the artifact. Spinnaker then deploys the new version as a canary -- a small percentage of traffic (usually 1-5%) is routed to the new version, while 95-99% continues to hit the old version. Automated analysis (using Kayenta, their canary analysis tool) compares error rates, latency percentiles, and business metrics between the canary and baseline. If the canary looks healthy after 30 minutes, traffic gradually shifts to the new version. If any metric degrades, the canary is automatically rolled back -- no human intervention required.

This system allows Netflix to deploy with confidence even during peak viewing hours. The key insight: **deployment safety comes from automated analysis, not from deploying only during "safe windows."** Netflix found that restricting deployments to business hours actually increased risk because it compressed all deployments into a smaller window, increasing the chance of interacting failures.

</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### 1.2 Resource Management

| Concept | Purpose | Key Detail |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Requests** | Guaranteed minimum resources | Used by scheduler to place pods. Pod won't be scheduled if no node has enough. |
| **Limits** | Maximum allowed resources | Pod is throttled (CPU) or OOM-killed (memory) if it exceeds limits. |
| **QoS Classes** | Priority during resource pressure | **Guaranteed** (requests=limits), **Burstable** (requests<limits), **BestEffort** (no requests/limits) |
| **LimitRanges** | Per-namespace defaults/constraints | Ensures every pod has resource requests even if developer forgets |
| **ResourceQuotas** | Per-namespace total caps | Prevents one team from consuming all cluster resources |

<Warning>
**The most common Kubernetes production incident: not setting memory limits.** A pod without a memory limit can consume all available memory on a node, causing the kubelet to OOM-kill other pods on the same node. Always set memory limits. For CPU, consider using only requests (not limits) to allow bursting -- CPU throttling is often worse than allowing brief spikes.
</Warning>

## 2. Kubernetes Workload Patterns

### 2.1 Workload Types

| Workload | Use Case | Key Behavior |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Deployment** | Stateless services (APIs, web servers) | Rolling updates, rollback, replica scaling |
| **StatefulSet** | Stateful services (databases, Kafka, Zookeeper) | Stable network identity, ordered deployment, persistent volumes |
| **DaemonSet** | One pod per node (log collectors, monitoring agents) | Automatically added to new nodes |
| **Job** | One-time tasks (migrations, batch processing) | Runs to completion, retries on failure |
| **CronJob** | Scheduled tasks | Creates Jobs on a cron schedule |

### 2.2 The Operator Pattern

Operators extend Kubernetes with custom logic. They combine Custom Resource Definitions (CRDs) with custom controllers that watch for changes to those resources and reconcile actual state with desired state.

**When to use Operators:**
- Managing stateful applications that need complex lifecycle operations (database failover, backup, scaling)
- Automating day-2 operations (certificate rotation, schema migration, version upgrades)
- Encoding operational knowledge into code ("when disk is 80% full, add a new node")

**Examples:** PostgreSQL Operator (Zalando), MongoDB Operator, Prometheus Operator, Cert-Manager

### 2.3 Helm vs Kustomize

| Aspect | Helm | Kustomize |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Approach** | Template engine (Go templates) | Overlay-based patching (no templates) |
| **Configuration** | `values.yaml` + templates | Base manifests + overlay patches |
| **Complexity** | Medium (learning Go templates) | Low (pure YAML) |
| **Ecosystem** | Massive chart repository (Artifact Hub) | Built into kubectl |
| **Best for** | Third-party applications, complex parameterization | Internal applications, environment-specific overrides |

<Tip>
**The pragmatic recommendation:** Use Helm for installing third-party software (Prometheus, cert-manager, ingress controllers) and Kustomize for your own applications. This gives you the Helm ecosystem for community software and the simplicity of Kustomize for your code.
</Tip>

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How would you design a Kubernetes platform for 50 engineering teams?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Cluster strategy:**
- **Multi-cluster over multi-tenant single cluster.** With 50 teams, a single cluster becomes a blast radius and governance nightmare. I would run 3-5 clusters: a shared "platform" cluster for common services (monitoring, logging, CI/CD), and 2-4 workload clusters (production, staging, plus regional if needed). Each cluster has its own RBAC, resource quotas, and network policies.

**Namespace strategy:**
- One namespace per team per environment. Team A gets `team-a-prod`, `team-a-staging`. This provides isolation (network policies, resource quotas), clear ownership, and easy cost attribution.

**Self-service guardrails:**
- **GitOps with ArgoCD:** Teams manage their own Kubernetes manifests in Git. ArgoCD syncs from Git to cluster. No `kubectl apply` in production -- all changes go through Git (auditable, reversible).
- **Policy enforcement:** OPA Gatekeeper or Kyverno policies that enforce: all pods must have resource requests/limits, no containers run as root, all images must come from an internal registry, no services exposed without TLS.
- **Templates:** Backstage templates that generate a new service with Dockerfile, Kubernetes manifests, CI/CD pipeline, monitoring dashboards, and namespace configuration. Time to first deployment: <30 minutes.

**Observability:**
- Centralized Prometheus + Grafana for metrics (with per-team dashboards auto-generated from service catalog)
- Centralized EFK/Loki for logs with per-namespace log retention policies
- Jaeger/Tempo for distributed tracing with auto-instrumented sidecars (OpenTelemetry collector as DaemonSet)

**Cost management:**
- Kubecost or OpenCost for per-namespace cost attribution
- Weekly cost reports per team
- Alerts for teams exceeding their budget by >20%

**Follow-up: "How do you handle cluster upgrades with 50 teams depending on it?"**

Rolling cluster upgrades with advance notice. We maintain n-1 Kubernetes version support. Announce upgrades 2 sprints in advance. Upgrade staging clusters first, give teams 1 sprint to validate. Then upgrade production with a canary approach: drain one node at a time, validate workloads, proceed. For breaking API changes (deprecated API versions), run Pluto or kubent to scan all manifests before upgrading.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 3. Kubernetes Networking

### 3.1 Service Types

| Service Type | Accessibility | Use Case |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **ClusterIP** | Internal only (within cluster) | Service-to-service communication |
| **NodePort** | External via node IP:port (30000-32767) | Development, debugging (not for production) |
| **LoadBalancer** | External via cloud load balancer | Production external services |
| **ExternalName** | DNS CNAME to external service | Mapping external services into cluster DNS |

### 3.2 Ingress and Service Mesh

| Component | Purpose | Popular Choices |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Ingress Controller** | Route external HTTP/HTTPS traffic to services | Nginx Ingress, Traefik, Envoy (Emissary) |
| **Service Mesh** | Service-to-service networking (mTLS, retries, circuit breaking) | Istio, Linkerd, Cilium Service Mesh |
| **Network Policies** | Firewall rules between pods | Calico, Cilium (eBPF-based) |

<Note>
**When do you need a service mesh?** If you have <20 services and trust your network, probably not. Service meshes add operational complexity (sidecar resource overhead, latency, debugging difficulty). They become valuable at scale (50+ services) when you need: mTLS between all services (zero-trust), advanced traffic management (canary deployments, traffic mirroring), or standardized observability (every service automatically reports latency/error metrics).
</Note>

# Part II -- Infrastructure as Code and GitOps

## 4. Terraform Deep Dive

### 4.1 State Management

<Warning>
**Terraform state is the most dangerous file in your infrastructure.** It contains a mapping between your configuration and real-world resources, and often includes sensitive values (database passwords, API keys). Never store state locally for shared infrastructure. Never commit state to Git.
</Warning>

**Best practices:**
- **Remote backend:** S3 + DynamoDB (AWS), GCS (GCP), Azure Blob + Table Storage
- **State locking:** Prevents concurrent modifications (DynamoDB lock for S3 backend)
- **Workspaces:** Separate state per environment (dev, staging, prod) or use directory-based separation
- **State encryption:** Enable server-side encryption on the backend (S3 bucket encryption, GCS default encryption)

### 4.2 IaC Tool Comparison

| Tool | Language | State | Cloud Support | Best For |
|------|----------|-------|--------------|---------|
| **Terraform** | HCL | Stateful (remote backend) | Multi-cloud | Multi-cloud, most teams |
| **Pulumi** | TypeScript, Python, Go | Stateful | Multi-cloud | Teams who prefer general-purpose languages |
| **CloudFormation** | JSON/YAML | AWS-managed | AWS only | AWS-only shops, tight AWS integration |
| **CDK** | TypeScript, Python | Generates CloudFormation | AWS (primarily) | AWS developers who want code, not YAML |
| **Crossplane** | Kubernetes YAML (CRDs) | Kubernetes-native | Multi-cloud | Teams already on Kubernetes, GitOps |

### 4.3 Module Design

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How do you structure Terraform for a multi-environment, multi-region deployment?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Directory structure:**
```
terraform/
├── modules/           # Reusable modules
│   ├── vpc/
│   ├── eks-cluster/
│   ├── rds-postgres/
│   └── service/       # Generic microservice module
├── environments/
│   ├── dev/
│   │   ├── main.tf    # Calls modules with dev config
│   │   ├── variables.tf
│   │   └── terraform.tfvars
│   ├── staging/
│   │   ├── main.tf
│   │   └── terraform.tfvars
│   └── prod/
│       ├── us-east-1/
│       │   ├── main.tf
│       │   └── terraform.tfvars
│       └── eu-west-1/
│           ├── main.tf
│           └── terraform.tfvars
└── global/            # Shared resources (IAM, DNS)
    ├── main.tf
    └── terraform.tfvars
```

**Principles:**
1. **Modules for reuse, environments for configuration.** Modules define *what* is created. Environment configs define *how* it is configured (instance sizes, replica counts, feature flags).
2. **Small state files.** Each environment/region has its own state. A change to dev cannot accidentally affect prod. State files are small enough to plan and apply in seconds.
3. **Minimize blast radius.** Global resources (IAM roles, DNS zones) are in separate state from per-region resources. A VPC change in us-east-1 cannot affect eu-west-1.
4. **Pin module versions.** Use Git tags for module versions. `source = "git::https://github.com/org/modules.git//vpc?ref=v1.2.0"`. This prevents accidental upgrades.

**Follow-up: "How do you handle secrets in Terraform?"**

Never put secrets in `terraform.tfvars` or state. Use: (1) AWS Secrets Manager / GCP Secret Manager to store secrets, and reference them with `data` sources. (2) Vault provider for dynamic secrets. (3) Environment variables for CI/CD (never committed). (4) `sensitive = true` on variables to prevent them from appearing in plan output.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 5. GitOps

### 5.1 Principles

GitOps treats Git as the single source of truth for infrastructure and application configuration. All changes go through Git, and an operator (ArgoCD, Flux) continuously reconciles the cluster state with what is declared in Git.

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Declarative** | Desired state described in YAML/Helm/Kustomize, not imperative scripts |
| **Versioned** | All changes committed to Git with history, authorship, and review |
| **Automated** | Operator applies changes automatically (no `kubectl apply`) |
| **Reconciled** | Operator continuously checks for drift between Git and cluster, and corrects it |

### 5.2 ArgoCD vs Flux

| Aspect | ArgoCD | Flux |
|--------|--------|------|
| **UI** | Rich web UI with visualization | CLI-first, minimal UI (Weave GitOps for UI) |
| **Architecture** | Centralized server + controller | Distributed toolkit (source, kustomize, helm controllers) |
| **Multi-cluster** | First-class support via ApplicationSets | Multi-cluster via Kustomization references |
| **Adoption** | More popular (larger community) | Deep CNCF integration, lighter weight |
| **Best for** | Teams that want visibility and a UI | Teams that prefer minimal components |

# Part III -- CI/CD and Release Engineering

## 6. CI/CD Pipeline Design

### 6.1 Pipeline Architecture

<Steps>
<Step title="Build">
Compile code, build container image, tag with Git SHA. Use multi-stage Docker builds to minimize image size. Cache build layers in CI (GitHub Actions cache, ECR cache).
</Step>
<Step title="Test">
Unit tests + integration tests. Fail fast on unit tests (seconds), run integration tests in parallel. For monorepos: only test affected packages (Nx, Turborepo, Bazel).
</Step>
<Step title="Security Scan">
Static analysis (Semgrep, CodeQL), dependency scanning (Snyk, Trivy), container image scanning (Trivy, Grype). Block merge if critical vulnerabilities found.
</Step>
<Step title="Build Artifact">
Push container image to registry (ECR, GCR, Harbor). Sign the image (Cosign). Generate SBOM (Syft).
</Step>
<Step title="Deploy to Staging">
Automatic deployment to staging environment. Run E2E tests against staging. Smoke tests for critical paths.
</Step>
<Step title="Deploy to Production">
Canary deployment (5% → 25% → 50% → 100%) with automated rollback triggers. Monitor error rates and latency during rollout.
</Step>
</Steps>

### 6.2 Deployment Strategies

| Strategy | How It Works | Risk Level | Rollback Speed |
|---------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Rolling** | Gradually replace old pods with new ones | Medium | Medium (new rolling update) |
| **Blue-Green** | Two full environments, switch traffic instantly | Low | Instant (switch back) |
| **Canary** | Small % of traffic to new version, gradually increase | Lowest | Instant (route to old) |
| **A/B Testing** | Route specific user segments to new version | Lowest | Instant |
| **Feature Flags** | New code deployed but disabled, enabled per-user/% | Lowest | Instant (disable flag) |

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: Design a CI/CD pipeline for a monorepo with 100 microservices">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Challenge:** Building and testing all 100 services on every PR is wasteful (30+ minute builds) and expensive. We need affected-based testing.

**Architecture:**

1. **Dependency graph:** Use a build tool that understands the monorepo dependency graph (Bazel, Nx, Turborepo). When a PR modifies service A, the tool determines which services are affected (A + services that depend on A's shared libraries).

2. **Affected-based CI:** Only build, test, and lint affected services. A PR that modifies a shared library might affect 20 services. A PR that modifies service A's business logic affects only A. This reduces median CI time from 30 minutes to 3-5 minutes.

3. **Build caching:** Remote build cache (Bazel remote cache, Turborepo remote cache). If the build input (code + dependencies) has not changed, use the cached artifact. This eliminates redundant builds across developers and CI runs.

4. **Container image strategy:** One Dockerfile per service. Build only affected service images. Push to a central registry with tags: `{service}:{git-sha}`. Image scanning runs post-push.

5. **Deployment:** Each service has its own ArgoCD Application. When a new image is pushed, update the image tag in the service's Kustomize overlay (via automated PR or direct commit to a deploy repo). ArgoCD detects the change and syncs.

6. **Parallelization:** CI jobs for independent services run in parallel. A PR affecting 10 services runs 10 build+test jobs concurrently. Total time = max(individual service CI times), not sum.

**Follow-up: "How do you handle shared library changes that could break downstream services?"**

Shared libraries are versioned. A PR that changes a shared library triggers CI for all downstream consumers. If a consumer's tests fail, the PR is blocked. For breaking changes: publish the new version alongside the old version, migrate consumers incrementally, then deprecate the old version.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 7. Release Engineering

### 7.1 Feature Flags

| Tool | Type | Best For |
|------|------|---------|
| **LaunchDarkly** | Commercial SaaS | Enterprise, complex targeting rules, scale |
| **Unleash** | Open-source (self-hosted) | Teams wanting full control, budget-conscious |
| **Flipt** | Open-source (Go) | Simple, fast, GitOps-compatible |
| **Flagsmith** | Open-source + hosted | Feature flags + remote config |
| **ConfigCat** | Commercial | Simple pricing, small-medium teams |

**Feature flag lifecycle:**

<Steps>
<Step title="Create flag (disabled)">
New code deployed behind a flag set to `false`. All users see the old behavior. The new code is in production but dormant.
</Step>
<Step title="Internal testing">
Enable for internal users (employee email domain). Validate in production with real data.
</Step>
<Step title="Beta rollout">
Enable for 5% of users. Monitor error rates, performance, and user feedback.
</Step>
<Step title="General availability">
Gradually increase to 25% → 50% → 100%. At each stage, monitor and confirm no regressions.
</Step>
<Step title="Cleanup">
Once 100% and stable for 2 weeks, remove the flag and the old code path. **This step is critical** -- stale feature flags accumulate as tech debt and make code increasingly hard to understand.
</Step>
</Steps>

## 8. Platform as a Product

### 8.1 DORA Metrics

The four key metrics from the DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) program that predict engineering team performance:

| Metric | Elite | High | Medium | Low |
|--------|-------|------|--------|-----|
| **Deployment Frequency** | On-demand (multiple/day) | Weekly-monthly | Monthly-biannually | <1 per 6 months |
| **Lead Time for Changes** | <1 hour | 1 day-1 week | 1-6 months | >6 months |
| **Change Failure Rate** | 0-15% | 16-30% | 16-30% | >30% |
| **Mean Time to Recovery** | <1 hour | <1 day | 1 day-1 week | >1 week |

<Note>
**DORA metrics matter because they are correlated with business outcomes.** Teams that score "Elite" on DORA metrics are twice as likely to exceed their organizational performance goals. The metrics are not just engineering vanity metrics -- they predict commercial success. Use them to measure platform effectiveness, not to judge individual engineers.
</Note>

### 8.2 Platform Team Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Symptom | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| **Ticket-driven ops** | Developers file tickets for every infrastructure change | Self-service tooling (Backstage, Terraform modules, templates) |
| **Ivory tower platform** | Platform team builds features developers do not need | Product thinking: interview internal customers, measure adoption |
| **Mandatory adoption** | Force teams onto the platform regardless of fit | Make the platform the path of least resistance, not a mandate |
| **No documentation** | Developers cannot use the platform without Slack messages | Treat docs as part of the product. If it is not documented, it does not exist. |
| **One-size-fits-all** | Same infrastructure for a batch job and a real-time API | Provide opinionated defaults with escape hatches for teams that need different |

## 9. Secrets Management

### 9.1 HashiCorp Vault Architecture

| Concept | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Secret Engine** | Generates or stores secrets (KV, AWS, database, PKI) |
| **Auth Method** | How clients authenticate to Vault (Kubernetes, OIDC, AppRole) |
| **Policy** | ACL rules (which paths a token can read/write) |
| **Lease** | Secrets have a TTL and must be renewed or re-fetched |
| **Dynamic Secrets** | Generated on demand (e.g., temporary database credentials) |

**Dynamic secrets** are Vault's killer feature: instead of storing a static database password that every service uses (and that is painful to rotate), Vault generates a unique, temporary credential for each request. The credential automatically expires after the lease period. If a credential is compromised, the blast radius is limited to one service's temporary access.

### 9.2 Secrets in Kubernetes

| Approach | Security Level | Complexity |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| **Kubernetes Secrets (base64)** | Low (not encrypted by default, visible in etcd) | Low |
| **Sealed Secrets (Bitnami)** | Medium (encrypted, safe in Git) | Medium |
| **External Secrets Operator** | High (syncs from Vault/AWS SM/GCP SM into K8s secrets) | Medium |
| **CSI Secrets Store Driver** | High (mounts secrets as volumes from external providers) | Medium-High |
| **Vault Agent Injector** | Highest (Vault sidecar fetches secrets at runtime) | High |

<Tip>
**The default recommendation for 2025:** External Secrets Operator (ESO) to sync secrets from your cloud provider's secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, GCP Secret Manager) into Kubernetes secrets. This gives you centralized secret management with native Kubernetes integration and minimal operational overhead. Graduate to Vault when you need dynamic secrets or complex access policies.
</Tip>

# Part V -- Observability and System Design

## 10. Observability Deep Dive

### 10.1 The Three Pillars (and Beyond)

| Pillar | What It Tells You | Tools |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **Metrics** | What is happening (quantitative) | Prometheus, Datadog, CloudWatch |
| **Logs** | Why it happened (detailed context) | ELK, Loki, CloudWatch Logs |
| **Traces** | Where it happened (request path across services) | Jaeger, Tempo, Zipkin, Datadog APM |

<Note>
**The three pillars are not enough.** Modern observability also includes: **profiling** (continuous profiling with Parca or Pyroscope to identify CPU/memory hotspots), **events** (deployment events, config changes correlated with metric changes), and **SLOs** (service level objectives that translate technical metrics into business-meaningful reliability targets).
</Note>

### 10.2 OpenTelemetry

OpenTelemetry (OTel) is the CNCF standard for instrumentation. It provides vendor-neutral SDKs and a Collector that decouples instrumentation from backend storage.

**Architecture:** Application → OTel SDK (auto-instrumented) → OTel Collector (receives, processes, exports) → Backend (Prometheus, Jaeger, Loki, Datadog, etc.)

**Why OTel matters:** Before OTel, switching from Datadog to Grafana Cloud meant re-instrumenting every service. With OTel, you change the Collector's exporter configuration. Your application code does not change.

### 10.3 Alerting Best Practices

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **Alert on symptoms, not causes** | Alert on "error rate > 1%" not "CPU > 80%". Users care about errors, not CPU. |
| **Use SLO-based alerting** | "Error budget burn rate is 10x normal" is more actionable than "500 errors detected" |
| **Avoid alert fatigue** | Every alert must be actionable. If an alert fires and the response is "ignore it," delete the alert. |
| **Tiered severity** | P1: revenue-impacting, wake someone up. P2: degraded experience, fix next business day. P3: investigate when convenient. |
| **Runbooks for every alert** | Every alert links to a runbook with diagnosis steps and remediation actions |

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How would you design an observability platform for a 200-service microservice architecture?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Metrics:**
- OpenTelemetry SDK in every service (auto-instrumentation for HTTP, gRPC, database calls)
- OTel Collector deployed as DaemonSet (one per node) -- collects, batches, and exports
- Prometheus for metric storage (or Thanos/Cortex for multi-cluster, long-term storage)
- Grafana for dashboards (auto-generated per-service dashboards from OTel conventions)

**Logs:**
- Structured JSON logging (not free-text). Every log includes: `timestamp`, `service`, `trace_id`, `level`, `message`.
- Collected by Fluent Bit DaemonSet → forwarded to Loki (or Elasticsearch)
- Retention: 7 days hot, 90 days cold storage (S3)
- Correlation: every log includes the trace_id, so you can click from a log line to the full distributed trace

**Traces:**
- Auto-instrumented with OTel (HTTP/gRPC propagate trace context headers automatically)
- Sampling: 100% for errors, 10% for successful requests (head-based sampling), tail-based sampling in Collector for high-latency requests
- Storage: Tempo (Grafana) or Jaeger
- Visualization: Grafana trace view with service map

**SLOs:**
- Define per-service SLOs: availability (% of requests returning non-5xx), latency (P99 < target)
- Error budget tracking: alert when burn rate exceeds threshold
- SLO dashboards visible to both engineering and product teams

**Cost management (critical at 200 services):**
- Metric cardinality management: limit high-cardinality labels (user_id is a label = cardinality explosion)
- Log volume management: sample debug logs, always log errors
- Trace sampling: 10% baseline reduces storage 10x with minimal observability loss

**Follow-up: "How do you prevent cardinality explosion?"**

Cardinality = unique combinations of label values. A metric with labels `{service, endpoint, status_code}` across 200 services, 50 endpoints each, 10 status codes = 100,000 time series. Add `user_id` (1M users) and you have 100 billion time series -- Prometheus will OOM. Prevention: (1) Never use unbounded values (user_id, request_id) as metric labels. (2) Use histogram buckets instead of exact values. (3) OTel Collector can drop/aggregate high-cardinality metrics before they reach Prometheus. (4) Set per-service cardinality limits in the Collector.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 11. Platform System Design Patterns

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="System Design: Design an Internal Deployment Platform">

**Requirements:**
- 50 teams deploy independently
- Support Kubernetes, serverless, and legacy VM deployments
- Automated canary analysis
- Self-service, no ticket required

**Architecture:**

1. **API layer:** REST/gRPC API that accepts deployment requests. Input: service name, version (container image tag or artifact ID), environment, deployment strategy.

2. **Pipeline orchestration:** Each deployment triggers a pipeline:
   - Pre-deploy checks (image exists, image scanned, tests passed)
   - Deploy canary (5% traffic)
   - Automated analysis (compare canary metrics to baseline for 15 minutes)
   - Progressive rollout (25% → 50% → 100%) or auto-rollback

3. **Multi-target deployers:**
   - Kubernetes deployer: updates ArgoCD Application or directly patches Deployment
   - Lambda deployer: updates function code via AWS API
   - VM deployer: triggers Ansible playbook for legacy services

4. **Audit and visibility:**
   - Every deployment recorded in a database (who, what, when, result)
   - Slack notifications for deployments and rollbacks
   - Dashboard showing deployment frequency, success rate, MTTR per team

5. **Self-service onboarding:**
   - Service registration in Backstage includes deployment configuration
   - Sensible defaults (canary with auto-rollback) with overrides for teams that need different strategies
