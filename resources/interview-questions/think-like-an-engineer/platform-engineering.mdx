# Platform Engineering & DevOps -- Infrastructure, Automation, and Developer Experience

*Platform engineering is the discipline of building the foundation that every other engineer ships on. A good platform team is invisible -- developers deploy without thinking about infrastructure, secrets rotate without anyone noticing, and incidents resolve before users feel them. A bad platform team is a bottleneck -- every deployment requires a ticket, every environment is a snowflake, and "works on my machine" is the most commonly heard phrase in standup. This chapter covers how senior platform engineers think about Kubernetes, infrastructure as code, CI/CD, observability, and the increasingly important discipline of developer experience.*

---

## Real-World Stories: Why This Matters

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="How Spotify Scaled to 2,000 Microservices with Backstage">

By 2019, Spotify had over 2,000 microservices, 350+ engineering teams, and a growing crisis: engineers spent more time searching for documentation, configuring infrastructure, and figuring out who owned what than they spent writing code. Onboarding a new engineer took weeks because they had to navigate a maze of Confluence pages, Slack channels, and tribal knowledge to understand how to deploy a service.

Spotify's answer was **Backstage** -- an open-source internal developer portal that became the single front door for all engineering activities. Backstage provides: a software catalog (every service, its owner, dependencies, documentation, and health status), templates for creating new services (with pre-configured CI/CD, monitoring, and access controls), and a plugin architecture that integrates every internal tool (PagerDuty, Kubernetes, Datadog, Jenkins).

The impact was transformative: time to create a new production service dropped from 2 weeks to 15 minutes. Developer satisfaction scores increased by 40%. Onboarding time dropped by 55%. And critically, the platform team stopped being the bottleneck -- developers could self-serve infrastructure through Backstage without filing tickets or waiting for approvals.

Spotify donated Backstage to the Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF), and by 2025 it has become the de facto standard for internal developer portals, adopted by companies including Netflix, Expedia, HP, and the US Department of Defense.

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="How Google's Borg Became Kubernetes -- and Changed Everything">

Google's internal cluster management system, Borg, had been orchestrating containers since 2003 -- a decade before Docker existed. Every Google service, from Search to Gmail to YouTube, ran on Borg. The system managed billions of containers across millions of machines.

In 2014, Google open-sourced a distillation of Borg's ideas as **Kubernetes**. The bet was strategic: if the industry adopted Kubernetes as the standard container orchestrator, Google Cloud Platform (GCP) would have a natural advantage since Kubernetes was designed by the engineers who built and operated the system it was based on.

The bet paid off beyond Google's expectations. Kubernetes became the universal abstraction layer for container orchestration, and it changed how the entire industry thinks about infrastructure. Instead of managing servers, teams manage desired state: "I want 3 replicas of this container, with 2 CPU and 4GB memory, accessible on port 8080." Kubernetes handles scheduling, scaling, self-healing, and networking.

But Kubernetes also introduced enormous complexity. Companies that adopted it without the operational maturity to manage it spent more time debugging Kubernetes than debugging their applications. The lesson that Google learned internally (and that many companies are still learning): **the power of Kubernetes comes from the platform layer built on top of it, not from Kubernetes itself.** Google's internal developers never interact with Borg directly -- they interact with higher-level abstractions. The companies that succeed with Kubernetes are those that build similar abstractions for their developers.

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="How Netflix Built a Zero-Downtime Deployment Machine">

Netflix deploys thousands of times per day across hundreds of microservices, and they do it while serving 250+ million subscribers. Their deployment philosophy: **every deploy is a canary.**

Netflix's deployment pipeline (Spinnaker, which they open-sourced) works like this: when a developer merges to main, a CI pipeline builds and tests the artifact. Spinnaker then deploys the new version as a canary -- a small percentage of traffic (usually 1-5%) is routed to the new version, while 95-99% continues to hit the old version. Automated analysis (using Kayenta, their canary analysis tool) compares error rates, latency percentiles, and business metrics between the canary and baseline. If the canary looks healthy after 30 minutes, traffic gradually shifts to the new version. If any metric degrades, the canary is automatically rolled back -- no human intervention required.

This system allows Netflix to deploy with confidence even during peak viewing hours. The key insight: **deployment safety comes from automated analysis, not from deploying only during "safe windows."** Netflix found that restricting deployments to business hours actually increased risk because it compressed all deployments into a smaller window, increasing the chance of interacting failures.

</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### 1.2 Resource Management

| Concept | Purpose | Key Detail |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Requests** | Guaranteed minimum resources | Used by scheduler to place pods. Pod won't be scheduled if no node has enough. |
| **Limits** | Maximum allowed resources | Pod is throttled (CPU) or OOM-killed (memory) if it exceeds limits. |
| **QoS Classes** | Priority during resource pressure | **Guaranteed** (requests=limits), **Burstable** (requests&lt;limits), **BestEffort** (no requests/limits) |
| **LimitRanges** | Per-namespace defaults/constraints | Ensures every pod has resource requests even if developer forgets |
| **ResourceQuotas** | Per-namespace total caps | Prevents one team from consuming all cluster resources |

<Warning>
**The most common Kubernetes production incident: not setting memory limits.** A pod without a memory limit can consume all available memory on a node, causing the kubelet to OOM-kill other pods on the same node. Always set memory limits. For CPU, consider using only requests (not limits) to allow bursting -- CPU throttling is often worse than allowing brief spikes.
</Warning>

## 2. Kubernetes Workload Patterns

### 2.1 Workload Types

| Workload | Use Case | Key Behavior |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Deployment** | Stateless services (APIs, web servers) | Rolling updates, rollback, replica scaling |
| **StatefulSet** | Stateful services (databases, Kafka, Zookeeper) | Stable network identity, ordered deployment, persistent volumes |
| **DaemonSet** | One pod per node (log collectors, monitoring agents) | Automatically added to new nodes |
| **Job** | One-time tasks (migrations, batch processing) | Runs to completion, retries on failure |
| **CronJob** | Scheduled tasks | Creates Jobs on a cron schedule |

### 2.2 The Operator Pattern

Operators extend Kubernetes with custom logic. They combine Custom Resource Definitions (CRDs) with custom controllers that watch for changes to those resources and reconcile actual state with desired state.

**When to use Operators:**
- Managing stateful applications that need complex lifecycle operations (database failover, backup, scaling)
- Automating day-2 operations (certificate rotation, schema migration, version upgrades)
- Encoding operational knowledge into code ("when disk is 80% full, add a new node")

**Examples:** PostgreSQL Operator (Zalando), MongoDB Operator, Prometheus Operator, Cert-Manager

### 2.3 Helm vs Kustomize

| Aspect | Helm | Kustomize |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Approach** | Template engine (Go templates) | Overlay-based patching (no templates) |
| **Configuration** | `values.yaml` + templates | Base manifests + overlay patches |
| **Complexity** | Medium (learning Go templates) | Low (pure YAML) |
| **Ecosystem** | Massive chart repository (Artifact Hub) | Built into kubectl |
| **Best for** | Third-party applications, complex parameterization | Internal applications, environment-specific overrides |

<Tip>
**The pragmatic recommendation:** Use Helm for installing third-party software (Prometheus, cert-manager, ingress controllers) and Kustomize for your own applications. This gives you the Helm ecosystem for community software and the simplicity of Kustomize for your code.
</Tip>

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How would you design a Kubernetes platform for 50 engineering teams?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Cluster strategy:**
- **Multi-cluster over multi-tenant single cluster.** With 50 teams, a single cluster becomes a blast radius and governance nightmare. I would run 3-5 clusters: a shared "platform" cluster for common services (monitoring, logging, CI/CD), and 2-4 workload clusters (production, staging, plus regional if needed). Each cluster has its own RBAC, resource quotas, and network policies.

**Namespace strategy:**
- One namespace per team per environment. Team A gets `team-a-prod`, `team-a-staging`. This provides isolation (network policies, resource quotas), clear ownership, and easy cost attribution.

**Self-service guardrails:**
- **GitOps with ArgoCD:** Teams manage their own Kubernetes manifests in Git. ArgoCD syncs from Git to cluster. No `kubectl apply` in production -- all changes go through Git (auditable, reversible).
- **Policy enforcement:** OPA Gatekeeper or Kyverno policies that enforce: all pods must have resource requests/limits, no containers run as root, all images must come from an internal registry, no services exposed without TLS.
- **Templates:** Backstage templates that generate a new service with Dockerfile, Kubernetes manifests, CI/CD pipeline, monitoring dashboards, and namespace configuration. Time to first deployment: &lt;30 minutes.

**Observability:**
- Centralized Prometheus + Grafana for metrics (with per-team dashboards auto-generated from service catalog)
- Centralized EFK/Loki for logs with per-namespace log retention policies
- Jaeger/Tempo for distributed tracing with auto-instrumented sidecars (OpenTelemetry collector as DaemonSet)

**Cost management:**
- Kubecost or OpenCost for per-namespace cost attribution
- Weekly cost reports per team
- Alerts for teams exceeding their budget by >20%

**Follow-up: "How do you handle cluster upgrades with 50 teams depending on it?"**

Rolling cluster upgrades with advance notice. We maintain n-1 Kubernetes version support. Announce upgrades 2 sprints in advance. Upgrade staging clusters first, give teams 1 sprint to validate. Then upgrade production with a canary approach: drain one node at a time, validate workloads, proceed. For breaking API changes (deprecated API versions), run Pluto or kubent to scan all manifests before upgrading.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 3. Kubernetes Networking

### 3.1 Service Types

| Service Type | Accessibility | Use Case |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **ClusterIP** | Internal only (within cluster) | Service-to-service communication |
| **NodePort** | External via node IP:port (30000-32767) | Development, debugging (not for production) |
| **LoadBalancer** | External via cloud load balancer | Production external services |
| **ExternalName** | DNS CNAME to external service | Mapping external services into cluster DNS |

### 3.2 Ingress and Service Mesh

| Component | Purpose | Popular Choices |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Ingress Controller** | Route external HTTP/HTTPS traffic to services | Nginx Ingress, Traefik, Envoy (Emissary) |
| **Service Mesh** | Service-to-service networking (mTLS, retries, circuit breaking) | Istio, Linkerd, Cilium Service Mesh |
| **Network Policies** | Firewall rules between pods | Calico, Cilium (eBPF-based) |

<Note>
**When do you need a service mesh?** If you have &lt;20 services and trust your network, probably not. Service meshes add operational complexity (sidecar resource overhead, latency, debugging difficulty). They become valuable at scale (50+ services) when you need: mTLS between all services (zero-trust), advanced traffic management (canary deployments, traffic mirroring), or standardized observability (every service automatically reports latency/error metrics).
</Note>

# Part II -- Infrastructure as Code and GitOps

## 4. Terraform Deep Dive

### 4.1 State Management

<Warning>
**Terraform state is the most dangerous file in your infrastructure.** It contains a mapping between your configuration and real-world resources, and often includes sensitive values (database passwords, API keys). Never store state locally for shared infrastructure. Never commit state to Git.
</Warning>

**Best practices:**
- **Remote backend:** S3 + DynamoDB (AWS), GCS (GCP), Azure Blob + Table Storage
- **State locking:** Prevents concurrent modifications (DynamoDB lock for S3 backend)
- **Workspaces:** Separate state per environment (dev, staging, prod) or use directory-based separation
- **State encryption:** Enable server-side encryption on the backend (S3 bucket encryption, GCS default encryption)

### 4.2 IaC Tool Comparison

| Tool | Language | State | Cloud Support | Best For |
|------|----------|-------|--------------|---------|
| **Terraform** | HCL | Stateful (remote backend) | Multi-cloud | Multi-cloud, most teams |
| **Pulumi** | TypeScript, Python, Go | Stateful | Multi-cloud | Teams who prefer general-purpose languages |
| **CloudFormation** | JSON/YAML | AWS-managed | AWS only | AWS-only shops, tight AWS integration |
| **CDK** | TypeScript, Python | Generates CloudFormation | AWS (primarily) | AWS developers who want code, not YAML |
| **Crossplane** | Kubernetes YAML (CRDs) | Kubernetes-native | Multi-cloud | Teams already on Kubernetes, GitOps |

### 4.3 Module Design

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How do you structure Terraform for a multi-environment, multi-region deployment?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Directory structure:**
```
terraform/
├── modules/           # Reusable modules
│   ├── vpc/
│   ├── eks-cluster/
│   ├── rds-postgres/
│   └── service/       # Generic microservice module
├── environments/
│   ├── dev/
│   │   ├── main.tf    # Calls modules with dev config
│   │   ├── variables.tf
│   │   └── terraform.tfvars
│   ├── staging/
│   │   ├── main.tf
│   │   └── terraform.tfvars
│   └── prod/
│       ├── us-east-1/
│       │   ├── main.tf
│       │   └── terraform.tfvars
│       └── eu-west-1/
│           ├── main.tf
│           └── terraform.tfvars
└── global/            # Shared resources (IAM, DNS)
    ├── main.tf
    └── terraform.tfvars
```

**Principles:**
1. **Modules for reuse, environments for configuration.** Modules define *what* is created. Environment configs define *how* it is configured (instance sizes, replica counts, feature flags).
2. **Small state files.** Each environment/region has its own state. A change to dev cannot accidentally affect prod. State files are small enough to plan and apply in seconds.
3. **Minimize blast radius.** Global resources (IAM roles, DNS zones) are in separate state from per-region resources. A VPC change in us-east-1 cannot affect eu-west-1.
4. **Pin module versions.** Use Git tags for module versions. `source = "git::https://github.com/org/modules.git//vpc?ref=v1.2.0"`. This prevents accidental upgrades.

**Follow-up: "How do you handle secrets in Terraform?"**

Never put secrets in `terraform.tfvars` or state. Use: (1) AWS Secrets Manager / GCP Secret Manager to store secrets, and reference them with `data` sources. (2) Vault provider for dynamic secrets. (3) Environment variables for CI/CD (never committed). (4) `sensitive = true` on variables to prevent them from appearing in plan output.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 5. GitOps

### 5.1 Principles

GitOps treats Git as the single source of truth for infrastructure and application configuration. All changes go through Git, and an operator (ArgoCD, Flux) continuously reconciles the cluster state with what is declared in Git.

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Declarative** | Desired state described in YAML/Helm/Kustomize, not imperative scripts |
| **Versioned** | All changes committed to Git with history, authorship, and review |
| **Automated** | Operator applies changes automatically (no `kubectl apply`) |
| **Reconciled** | Operator continuously checks for drift between Git and cluster, and corrects it |

### 5.2 ArgoCD vs Flux

| Aspect | ArgoCD | Flux |
|--------|--------|------|
| **UI** | Rich web UI with visualization | CLI-first, minimal UI (Weave GitOps for UI) |
| **Architecture** | Centralized server + controller | Distributed toolkit (source, kustomize, helm controllers) |
| **Multi-cluster** | First-class support via ApplicationSets | Multi-cluster via Kustomization references |
| **Adoption** | More popular (larger community) | Deep CNCF integration, lighter weight |
| **Best for** | Teams that want visibility and a UI | Teams that prefer minimal components |

# Part III -- CI/CD and Release Engineering

## 6. CI/CD Pipeline Design

### 6.1 Pipeline Architecture

<Steps>
<Step title="Build">
Compile code, build container image, tag with Git SHA. Use multi-stage Docker builds to minimize image size. Cache build layers in CI (GitHub Actions cache, ECR cache).
</Step>
<Step title="Test">
Unit tests + integration tests. Fail fast on unit tests (seconds), run integration tests in parallel. For monorepos: only test affected packages (Nx, Turborepo, Bazel).
</Step>
<Step title="Security Scan">
Static analysis (Semgrep, CodeQL), dependency scanning (Snyk, Trivy), container image scanning (Trivy, Grype). Block merge if critical vulnerabilities found.
</Step>
<Step title="Build Artifact">
Push container image to registry (ECR, GCR, Harbor). Sign the image (Cosign). Generate SBOM (Syft).
</Step>
<Step title="Deploy to Staging">
Automatic deployment to staging environment. Run E2E tests against staging. Smoke tests for critical paths.
</Step>
<Step title="Deploy to Production">
Canary deployment (5% → 25% → 50% → 100%) with automated rollback triggers. Monitor error rates and latency during rollout.
</Step>
</Steps>

### 6.2 Deployment Strategies

| Strategy | How It Works | Risk Level | Rollback Speed |
|---------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Rolling** | Gradually replace old pods with new ones | Medium | Medium (new rolling update) |
| **Blue-Green** | Two full environments, switch traffic instantly | Low | Instant (switch back) |
| **Canary** | Small % of traffic to new version, gradually increase | Lowest | Instant (route to old) |
| **A/B Testing** | Route specific user segments to new version | Lowest | Instant |
| **Feature Flags** | New code deployed but disabled, enabled per-user/% | Lowest | Instant (disable flag) |

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: Design a CI/CD pipeline for a monorepo with 100 microservices">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Challenge:** Building and testing all 100 services on every PR is wasteful (30+ minute builds) and expensive. We need affected-based testing.

**Architecture:**

1. **Dependency graph:** Use a build tool that understands the monorepo dependency graph (Bazel, Nx, Turborepo). When a PR modifies service A, the tool determines which services are affected (A + services that depend on A's shared libraries).

2. **Affected-based CI:** Only build, test, and lint affected services. A PR that modifies a shared library might affect 20 services. A PR that modifies service A's business logic affects only A. This reduces median CI time from 30 minutes to 3-5 minutes.

3. **Build caching:** Remote build cache (Bazel remote cache, Turborepo remote cache). If the build input (code + dependencies) has not changed, use the cached artifact. This eliminates redundant builds across developers and CI runs.

4. **Container image strategy:** One Dockerfile per service. Build only affected service images. Push to a central registry with tags: `{service}:{git-sha}`. Image scanning runs post-push.

5. **Deployment:** Each service has its own ArgoCD Application. When a new image is pushed, update the image tag in the service's Kustomize overlay (via automated PR or direct commit to a deploy repo). ArgoCD detects the change and syncs.

6. **Parallelization:** CI jobs for independent services run in parallel. A PR affecting 10 services runs 10 build+test jobs concurrently. Total time = max(individual service CI times), not sum.

**Follow-up: "How do you handle shared library changes that could break downstream services?"**

Shared libraries are versioned. A PR that changes a shared library triggers CI for all downstream consumers. If a consumer's tests fail, the PR is blocked. For breaking changes: publish the new version alongside the old version, migrate consumers incrementally, then deprecate the old version.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 7. Release Engineering

### 7.1 Feature Flags

| Tool | Type | Best For |
|------|------|---------|
| **LaunchDarkly** | Commercial SaaS | Enterprise, complex targeting rules, scale |
| **Unleash** | Open-source (self-hosted) | Teams wanting full control, budget-conscious |
| **Flipt** | Open-source (Go) | Simple, fast, GitOps-compatible |
| **Flagsmith** | Open-source + hosted | Feature flags + remote config |
| **ConfigCat** | Commercial | Simple pricing, small-medium teams |

**Feature flag lifecycle:**

<Steps>
<Step title="Create flag (disabled)">
New code deployed behind a flag set to `false`. All users see the old behavior. The new code is in production but dormant.
</Step>
<Step title="Internal testing">
Enable for internal users (employee email domain). Validate in production with real data.
</Step>
<Step title="Beta rollout">
Enable for 5% of users. Monitor error rates, performance, and user feedback.
</Step>
<Step title="General availability">
Gradually increase to 25% → 50% → 100%. At each stage, monitor and confirm no regressions.
</Step>
<Step title="Cleanup">
Once 100% and stable for 2 weeks, remove the flag and the old code path. **This step is critical** -- stale feature flags accumulate as tech debt and make code increasingly hard to understand.
</Step>
</Steps>

## 8. Platform as a Product

### 8.1 DORA Metrics

The four key metrics from the DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) program that predict engineering team performance:

| Metric | Elite | High | Medium | Low |
|--------|-------|------|--------|-----|
| **Deployment Frequency** | On-demand (multiple/day) | Weekly-monthly | Monthly-biannually | &lt;1 per 6 months |
| **Lead Time for Changes** | &lt;1 hour | 1 day-1 week | 1-6 months | >6 months |
| **Change Failure Rate** | 0-15% | 16-30% | 16-30% | >30% |
| **Mean Time to Recovery** | &lt;1 hour | &lt;1 day | 1 day-1 week | >1 week |

<Note>
**DORA metrics matter because they are correlated with business outcomes.** Teams that score "Elite" on DORA metrics are twice as likely to exceed their organizational performance goals. The metrics are not just engineering vanity metrics -- they predict commercial success. Use them to measure platform effectiveness, not to judge individual engineers.
</Note>

### 8.2 Platform Team Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Symptom | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| **Ticket-driven ops** | Developers file tickets for every infrastructure change | Self-service tooling (Backstage, Terraform modules, templates) |
| **Ivory tower platform** | Platform team builds features developers do not need | Product thinking: interview internal customers, measure adoption |
| **Mandatory adoption** | Force teams onto the platform regardless of fit | Make the platform the path of least resistance, not a mandate |
| **No documentation** | Developers cannot use the platform without Slack messages | Treat docs as part of the product. If it is not documented, it does not exist. |
| **One-size-fits-all** | Same infrastructure for a batch job and a real-time API | Provide opinionated defaults with escape hatches for teams that need different |

## 9. Secrets Management

### 9.1 HashiCorp Vault Architecture

| Concept | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Secret Engine** | Generates or stores secrets (KV, AWS, database, PKI) |
| **Auth Method** | How clients authenticate to Vault (Kubernetes, OIDC, AppRole) |
| **Policy** | ACL rules (which paths a token can read/write) |
| **Lease** | Secrets have a TTL and must be renewed or re-fetched |
| **Dynamic Secrets** | Generated on demand (e.g., temporary database credentials) |

**Dynamic secrets** are Vault's killer feature: instead of storing a static database password that every service uses (and that is painful to rotate), Vault generates a unique, temporary credential for each request. The credential automatically expires after the lease period. If a credential is compromised, the blast radius is limited to one service's temporary access.

### 9.2 Secrets in Kubernetes

| Approach | Security Level | Complexity |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| **Kubernetes Secrets (base64)** | Low (not encrypted by default, visible in etcd) | Low |
| **Sealed Secrets (Bitnami)** | Medium (encrypted, safe in Git) | Medium |
| **External Secrets Operator** | High (syncs from Vault/AWS SM/GCP SM into K8s secrets) | Medium |
| **CSI Secrets Store Driver** | High (mounts secrets as volumes from external providers) | Medium-High |
| **Vault Agent Injector** | Highest (Vault sidecar fetches secrets at runtime) | High |

<Tip>
**The default recommendation for 2025:** External Secrets Operator (ESO) to sync secrets from your cloud provider's secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, GCP Secret Manager) into Kubernetes secrets. This gives you centralized secret management with native Kubernetes integration and minimal operational overhead. Graduate to Vault when you need dynamic secrets or complex access policies.
</Tip>

## 10. Observability Deep Dive

### 10.1 The Three Pillars (and Beyond)

| Pillar | What It Tells You | Tools |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **Metrics** | What is happening (quantitative) | Prometheus, Datadog, CloudWatch |
| **Logs** | Why it happened (detailed context) | ELK, Loki, CloudWatch Logs |
| **Traces** | Where it happened (request path across services) | Jaeger, Tempo, Zipkin, Datadog APM |

<Note>
**The three pillars are not enough.** Modern observability also includes: **profiling** (continuous profiling with Parca or Pyroscope to identify CPU/memory hotspots), **events** (deployment events, config changes correlated with metric changes), and **SLOs** (service level objectives that translate technical metrics into business-meaningful reliability targets).
</Note>

### 10.2 OpenTelemetry

OpenTelemetry (OTel) is the CNCF standard for instrumentation. It provides vendor-neutral SDKs and a Collector that decouples instrumentation from backend storage.

**Architecture:** Application → OTel SDK (auto-instrumented) → OTel Collector (receives, processes, exports) → Backend (Prometheus, Jaeger, Loki, Datadog, etc.)

**Why OTel matters:** Before OTel, switching from Datadog to Grafana Cloud meant re-instrumenting every service. With OTel, you change the Collector's exporter configuration. Your application code does not change.

### 10.3 Alerting Best Practices

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| **Alert on symptoms, not causes** | Alert on "error rate > 1%" not "CPU > 80%". Users care about errors, not CPU. |
| **Use SLO-based alerting** | "Error budget burn rate is 10x normal" is more actionable than "500 errors detected" |
| **Avoid alert fatigue** | Every alert must be actionable. If an alert fires and the response is "ignore it," delete the alert. |
| **Tiered severity** | P1: revenue-impacting, wake someone up. P2: degraded experience, fix next business day. P3: investigate when convenient. |
| **Runbooks for every alert** | Every alert links to a runbook with diagnosis steps and remediation actions |

<Accordion title="Interview: How would you design an observability platform for a 200-service microservice architecture?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Metrics:**
- OpenTelemetry SDK in every service (auto-instrumentation for HTTP, gRPC, database calls)
- OTel Collector deployed as DaemonSet (one per node) -- collects, batches, and exports
- Prometheus for metric storage (or Thanos/Cortex for multi-cluster, long-term storage)
- Grafana for dashboards (auto-generated per-service dashboards from OTel conventions)

**Logs:**
- Structured JSON logging (not free-text). Every log includes: `timestamp`, `service`, `trace_id`, `level`, `message`.
- Collected by Fluent Bit DaemonSet → forwarded to Loki (or Elasticsearch)
- Retention: 7 days hot, 90 days cold storage (S3)
- Correlation: every log includes the trace_id, so you can click from a log line to the full distributed trace

**Traces:**
- Auto-instrumented with OTel (HTTP/gRPC propagate trace context headers automatically)
- Sampling: 100% for errors, 10% for successful requests (head-based sampling), tail-based sampling in Collector for high-latency requests
- Storage: Tempo (Grafana) or Jaeger
- Visualization: Grafana trace view with service map

**SLOs:**
- Define per-service SLOs: availability (% of requests returning non-5xx), latency (P99 &lt; target)
- Error budget tracking: alert when burn rate exceeds threshold
- SLO dashboards visible to both engineering and product teams

**Cost management (critical at 200 services):**
- Metric cardinality management: limit high-cardinality labels (user_id is a label = cardinality explosion)
- Log volume management: sample debug logs, always log errors
- Trace sampling: 10% baseline reduces storage 10x with minimal observability loss

**Follow-up: "How do you prevent cardinality explosion?"**

Cardinality = unique combinations of label values. A metric with labels `{service, endpoint, status_code}` across 200 services, 50 endpoints each, 10 status codes = 100,000 time series. Add `user_id` (1M users) and you have 100 billion time series -- Prometheus will OOM. Prevention: (1) Never use unbounded values (user_id, request_id) as metric labels. (2) Use histogram buckets instead of exact values. (3) OTel Collector can drop/aggregate high-cardinality metrics before they reach Prometheus. (4) Set per-service cardinality limits in the Collector.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 12. Kubernetes Deep Dive Questions

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: A pod is stuck in CrashLoopBackOff. Walk me through your debugging process.">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

CrashLoopBackOff means the container starts, crashes, Kubernetes restarts it, it crashes again, and the backoff delay increases exponentially (10s, 20s, 40s, up to 5 minutes). This is the most common Kubernetes issue engineers encounter.

**Step-by-step diagnosis:**

<Steps>
<Step title="Check pod events">
`kubectl describe pod <name>` -- look at the Events section at the bottom. Common findings:
- **OOMKilled**: Container exceeded memory limit. Fix: increase memory limit or fix the memory leak.
- **Error**: Container exited with non-zero code. Fix: check application logs.
- **ImagePullBackOff mixed in**: Wrong image tag or registry auth failure.
</Step>
<Step title="Check container logs">
`kubectl logs <pod> --previous` -- the `--previous` flag shows logs from the last crashed instance. Without it, you might see logs from a container that just started and has not crashed yet.
</Step>
<Step title="Check resource limits">
`kubectl get pod <name> -o yaml | grep -A 5 resources` -- if memory limit is 128Mi and the application needs 256Mi, it will be OOMKilled every time.
</Step>
<Step title="Check liveness/readiness probes">
A misconfigured liveness probe (wrong port, wrong path, timeout too short) will cause Kubernetes to kill a healthy container. Check `livenessProbe` in the pod spec. A Java application that takes 30 seconds to start with a liveness probe that has `initialDelaySeconds: 5` will be killed before it is ready.
</Step>
<Step title="Check configuration and secrets">
Missing ConfigMap or Secret references cause immediate crash. `kubectl describe pod` will show `CreateContainerConfigError` if a referenced ConfigMap/Secret does not exist.
</Step>
<Step title="Exec into the container (if it stays up long enough)">
`kubectl exec -it <pod> -- /bin/sh` -- sometimes the container runs for a few seconds before crashing. If you can exec in during that window, check filesystem state, environment variables, and connectivity to dependencies.
</Step>
</Steps>

**The diagnostic decision tree a senior engineer uses:**

| Symptom in `kubectl describe` | Likely Cause | Fix |
|------------------------------|-------------|-----|
| Exit Code 137 | OOMKilled (SIGKILL from kernel) | Increase memory limit or fix memory leak |
| Exit Code 1 | Application error at startup | Check logs: missing config, DB connection failed, bad env var |
| Exit Code 0 then restart | Container completed successfully but restartPolicy is Always | Change to restartPolicy: OnFailure (for Jobs) or fix the entrypoint |
| No events, just CrashLoopBackOff | Very fast crash before logging | Add a sleep to the entrypoint to buy time for debugging, or check the image's CMD/ENTRYPOINT |
| CreateContainerConfigError | Missing ConfigMap/Secret | Verify the ConfigMap/Secret exists in the correct namespace |

**Follow-up: "The pod keeps getting OOMKilled at 512Mi but works fine locally with 256MB. Why?"**

Several reasons: (1) JVM-based apps: the JVM uses more memory than the heap (`-Xmx`). Native memory, thread stacks, metaspace, and direct buffers add 1.5-2x overhead. Set `-Xmx` to ~75% of the container memory limit. (2) Sidecar containers (Istio envoy, log collector) share the pod's memory limit. A 50MB envoy sidecar eats into the 512Mi budget. (3) Memory metrics: `kubectl top pod` shows RSS, but the kernel tracks total memory including page cache. The OOM killer uses the total, not RSS.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Interview: How do you implement zero-downtime deployments in Kubernetes?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

Zero-downtime deployment means no request is dropped and no user sees an error during a deployment. This requires coordination between the deployment strategy, pod lifecycle, and load balancing.

**The four requirements:**

1. **Rolling update strategy** with proper `maxSurge` and `maxUnavailable`:
```yaml
strategy:
  type: RollingUpdate
  rollingUpdate:
    maxSurge: 25%        # Create new pods before killing old ones
    maxUnavailable: 0     # Never have fewer than desired replicas
```
Setting `maxUnavailable: 0` ensures the old pods stay alive until new pods are ready.

2. **Readiness probe** that gates traffic:
```yaml
readinessProbe:
  httpGet:
    path: /health/ready
    port: 8080
  initialDelaySeconds: 5
  periodSeconds: 5
  failureThreshold: 3
```
A pod only receives traffic after the readiness probe succeeds. This prevents routing requests to a container that is still initializing.

3. **Graceful shutdown** with `preStop` hook:
```yaml
lifecycle:
  preStop:
    exec:
      command: ["/bin/sh", "-c", "sleep 10"]
```
**Why the sleep matters:** When Kubernetes decides to terminate a pod, two things happen simultaneously: (a) the pod is removed from the Service endpoints, and (b) SIGTERM is sent to the container. Due to propagation delays, the load balancer may still send traffic to the pod for a few seconds after it receives SIGTERM. The `preStop` sleep gives the endpoints update time to propagate before the application starts shutting down.

4. **Pod Disruption Budgets (PDB)** for voluntary disruptions:
```yaml
apiVersion: policy/v1
kind: PodDisruptionBudget
metadata:
  name: my-app-pdb
spec:
  minAvailable: 2
  selector:
    matchLabels:
      app: my-app
```
PDBs ensure that node drains (during cluster upgrades or autoscaling) do not take down all replicas simultaneously.

**The sequence for zero-downtime:**
1. New pod created → passes readiness probe → added to Service endpoints → starts receiving traffic
2. Old pod removed from Service endpoints → `preStop` hook executes (sleep 10s for propagation) → SIGTERM sent → application finishes in-flight requests → container exits
3. At no point are there zero ready pods serving traffic

**Follow-up: "What about database migrations during deployment?"**

Database migrations must be backward compatible. The old version of the application runs alongside the new version during rolling updates. If the new version requires a schema change that breaks the old version, the migration must be split:
1. Deploy migration that adds new columns/tables (backward compatible)
2. Deploy application version that uses new schema
3. Deploy cleanup migration that removes old columns (after old version is fully gone)

This is the "expand and contract" pattern. Never deploy a breaking migration and new application code in the same release.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Interview: Your Kubernetes cluster costs have doubled in 3 months. How do you investigate and optimize?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Step 1: Identify where the money goes.**

Kubernetes costs come from: compute (node instances), storage (PVs, EBS volumes), networking (load balancers, cross-AZ traffic), and managed Kubernetes control plane fees.

Use a cost visibility tool: Kubecost (open-source), OpenCost (CNCF), or cloud-native tools (AWS Cost Explorer with EKS tags, GKE cost allocation).

**Step 2: Common cost wastes and fixes:**

| Waste | How to Detect | Fix | Typical Savings |
|-------|-------------|-----|----------------|
| **Over-provisioned requests** | `kubectl top pods` shows actual usage far below requests | Right-size requests using VPA recommendations | 30-50% |
| **Idle nodes** | Nodes with &lt;30% CPU/memory utilization | Enable Cluster Autoscaler or Karpenter, set appropriate min/max | 20-40% |
| **On-demand instances for non-critical** | All nodes are on-demand | Use spot/preemptible instances for stateless workloads (with PDB for graceful handling) | 60-80% on those nodes |
| **Oversized PVs** | 100GB PV with 5GB used | Resize PVs (most CSI drivers support online resize). Use dynamic provisioning with appropriate size classes. | Variable |
| **Cross-AZ data transfer** | High networking bill | Use topology-aware routing (`topologySpreadConstraints`), place related services in same AZ | 10-30% of networking |
| **Forgotten workloads** | Namespaces with deployments nobody owns | Audit with labels and ownership metadata. Delete abandoned workloads. | Variable but often significant |

**Step 3: Structural optimizations:**

- **Karpenter over Cluster Autoscaler** (on AWS): Karpenter provisions right-sized nodes per workload (instead of fixed node groups). It can bin-pack different instance types and sizes, mixing spot and on-demand, to minimize waste.
- **VPA (Vertical Pod Autoscaler)** in recommendation mode: Let VPA observe actual resource usage for 2 weeks, then apply its right-sizing recommendations. Most teams over-provision by 2-5x.
- **HPA (Horizontal Pod Autoscaler)** with custom metrics: Scale on business metrics (requests per second, queue depth) not just CPU. This prevents scaling to 50 replicas for a CPU spike caused by a one-time batch job.
- **KEDA (Kubernetes Event-Driven Autoscaler)** for scale-to-zero: For workloads with intermittent traffic (internal tools, cron-triggered services), KEDA can scale replicas to zero when idle.

**Follow-up: "How do you prevent costs from creeping back up?"**

Three mechanisms: (1) **Resource quotas per namespace** -- each team has a compute budget they cannot exceed. (2) **Cost alerts** -- Kubecost alerts when namespace spend exceeds threshold. (3) **LimitRanges** with defaults -- every pod gets sensible resource requests even if the developer forgets to set them. (4) **Regular reviews** -- monthly cost review per team, comparing actual spend to budget, with the platform team providing right-sizing recommendations.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: You need to manage infrastructure for 3 environments (dev, staging, prod) across 2 regions. How do you structure your Terraform?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Principle: minimize blast radius, maximize reuse.**

**Option A: Directory-based separation (recommended for most teams)**
```
terraform/
├── modules/                    # Reusable, versioned modules
│   ├── networking/             # VPC, subnets, NAT, security groups
│   │   ├── main.tf
│   │   ├── variables.tf
│   │   └── outputs.tf
│   ├── compute/                # EKS/GKE cluster, node groups
│   ├── database/               # RDS/CloudSQL, replicas
│   └── monitoring/             # Prometheus, Grafana, alerting
├── environments/
│   ├── dev/
│   │   └── us-east-1/
│   │       ├── main.tf         # Calls modules with dev config
│   │       ├── backend.tf      # S3 backend for this env/region
│   │       └── terraform.tfvars
│   ├── staging/
│   │   └── us-east-1/
│   │       ├── main.tf
│   │       ├── backend.tf
│   │       └── terraform.tfvars
│   └── prod/
│       ├── us-east-1/
│       │   ├── main.tf
│       │   ├── backend.tf
│       │   └── terraform.tfvars
│       └── eu-west-1/
│           ├── main.tf
│           ├── backend.tf
│           └── terraform.tfvars
└── global/                     # IAM roles, DNS zones, org-wide
    ├── main.tf
    └── backend.tf
```

**Why this structure works:**
- Each environment/region has its own state file. A `terraform apply` in dev cannot accidentally affect prod.
- Modules are shared and versioned. A VPC module used in dev is the same module used in prod (with different variable values).
- Environment-specific config lives in `terraform.tfvars` (instance sizes, replica counts, feature flags).
- Global resources (IAM, DNS) are separate state -- they rarely change and affect all environments.

**Option B: Workspace-based (simpler, more risk)**
Use Terraform workspaces (`terraform workspace select prod`) with conditional logic:
```hcl
locals {
  instance_type = terraform.workspace == "prod" ? "m5.xlarge" : "t3.medium"
  replicas      = terraform.workspace == "prod" ? 3 : 1
}
```
Simpler structure but riskier: forgetting to switch workspaces before applying can deploy prod config to dev.

**My recommendation:** Directory-based for anything with production workloads. Workspaces only for personal sandboxes or very small teams.

**Follow-up: "How do you handle module versioning so a change to the networking module doesn't break prod?"**

Pin module versions using Git tags:
```hcl
module "vpc" {
  source = "git::https://github.com/org/tf-modules.git//networking?ref=v2.3.1"
}
```
Workflow: develop module change → test in dev with `ref=feature-branch` → merge and tag `v2.4.0` → update staging to `v2.4.0` → validate → update prod to `v2.4.0`. Prod always lags staging by at least one deploy cycle.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Interview: How do you handle Terraform state drift -- when someone changes infrastructure manually?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

State drift occurs when the real infrastructure diverges from what Terraform thinks exists. This happens when someone manually changes a resource via the AWS console, another tool modifies infrastructure, or a previous `terraform apply` partially failed.

**Detection:**
- `terraform plan` shows changes you did not expect ("this security group rule will be removed" -- someone added it manually)
- Scheduled drift detection: run `terraform plan` in CI on a schedule (daily) and alert on any detected changes
- Tools: Spacelift, Env0, and Terraform Cloud have built-in drift detection

**Resolution strategies:**

| Scenario | Action | Command |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Manual change should be reverted | Apply Terraform to overwrite the manual change | `terraform apply` (Terraform restores desired state) |
| Manual change should be kept | Import into Terraform state and update config | `terraform import` + update `.tf` files to match |
| Resource exists but is not in Terraform | Import it | `terraform import aws_security_group.example sg-123456` |
| Resource in state but deleted externally | Remove from state | `terraform state rm aws_instance.example` |

**Prevention:**
1. **Lock down console access** -- use IAM policies that grant read-only console access for most engineers. Changes go through Terraform PRs.
2. **Tagging convention** -- Terraform-managed resources get a tag `managed_by = "terraform"`. Alerts fire if a tagged resource is modified outside Terraform.
3. **Policy as code** -- OPA (Open Policy Agent) or Sentinel policies in Terraform Cloud can enforce that certain resource types can only be created via Terraform.
4. **Culture** -- make Terraform the path of least resistance. If manually creating a security group is faster than a Terraform PR, the Terraform workflow needs improvement.

**Follow-up: "What about emergency changes that can't wait for a PR?"**

For genuine emergencies (production down, need to open a port NOW): make the manual change, fix the incident, then immediately file a follow-up PR that imports the change into Terraform. The rule is not "never touch the console" -- it is "every console change gets codified within 24 hours." Uncodified manual changes accumulate as infrastructure tech debt and will eventually cause a Terraform plan to break at the worst possible moment.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 14. CI/CD and Release Engineering Deep Dive

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How would you implement canary deployments with automated rollback?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**Architecture:**

1. **Traffic splitting** -- Use Argo Rollouts (Kubernetes), Istio VirtualService, or AWS ALB weighted target groups to split traffic:
   - 95% → stable (current production version)
   - 5% → canary (new version)

2. **Metric collection** -- The canary and stable both emit metrics to Prometheus:
   - Error rate (5xx responses / total responses)
   - Latency (P50, P95, P99)
   - Business metrics (conversion rate, if applicable)

3. **Automated analysis** -- A canary analysis tool (Kayenta from Netflix/Spinnaker, or Argo Rollouts analysis) compares canary metrics to stable metrics over a window (e.g., 15 minutes):
   - **Pass:** Canary error rate is within 0.5% of stable, latency P99 is within 10% → promote to 25%
   - **Marginal:** Canary is slightly worse but within tolerance → extend analysis window
   - **Fail:** Canary error rate is 2x stable, or latency P99 is 50% higher → automatic rollback

4. **Progressive rollout** (if analysis passes):
   - 5% → 15 min analysis → 25% → 15 min → 50% → 15 min → 100%
   - At each step, re-run analysis. Failure at any step triggers rollback.

5. **Rollback mechanism:**
   - Argo Rollouts: sets canary weight to 0%, scales down canary pods
   - Istio: updates VirtualService to route 100% to stable
   - ALB: removes canary target group from the listener rule

**Argo Rollouts example:**
```yaml
apiVersion: argoproj.io/v1alpha1
kind: Rollout
spec:
  strategy:
    canary:
      steps:
      - setWeight: 5
      - pause: {duration: 15m}
      - analysis:
          templates:
          - templateName: error-rate-check
      - setWeight: 25
      - pause: {duration: 15m}
      - analysis:
          templates:
          - templateName: error-rate-check
      - setWeight: 50
      - pause: {duration: 15m}
      - setWeight: 100
```

**Key trade-offs:**
- **Traffic-based vs replica-based canary:** Traffic splitting (Istio/ALB) gives precise control (exactly 5% of users). Replica-based (1 canary pod out of 20 = 5%) is simpler but less precise.
- **Analysis window length:** Longer windows catch slow-burn regressions but delay deployments. 15 minutes per step is a good default. Critical services might use 30 minutes.
- **Metric selection:** Error rate alone misses latency regressions. Latency alone misses correctness issues. Use at least error rate + latency P99. Add business metrics for critical flows.

**Follow-up: "What happens if the canary looks fine at 5% but fails at 50%?"**

This happens when bugs depend on traffic patterns that only appear at scale (race conditions, resource contention, hot partitions). The stepped approach catches this: 5% → 25% → 50% each has its own analysis. If the 50% step fails, traffic is routed back to 100% stable within seconds. Monitoring the canary at each step with increasing load is the whole point of progressive delivery.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Interview: How do you handle database migrations in a CI/CD pipeline without downtime?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**The fundamental constraint:** During a rolling deployment, old and new application versions run simultaneously. Both versions must be able to work with the database schema. This means every migration must be **backward compatible**.

**The Expand-Contract pattern:**

<Steps>
<Step title="Expand: Add new structure (backward compatible)">
Add new columns, tables, or indexes. Do NOT remove or rename anything the old version uses.

Example: Renaming `user_name` to `display_name`:
- Migration 1: `ALTER TABLE users ADD COLUMN display_name VARCHAR(255);`
- Migration 1: Backfill: `UPDATE users SET display_name = user_name WHERE display_name IS NULL;`
- Migration 1: Add trigger to keep both columns in sync during transition
</Step>
<Step title="Migrate: Deploy new application version">
New version reads from `display_name`, writes to both `display_name` and `user_name` (for backward compatibility). Old version still reads from `user_name`.
</Step>
<Step title="Contract: Remove old structure (after old version is fully retired)">
Migration 2 (deployed after old version is gone): `ALTER TABLE users DROP COLUMN user_name;`
</Step>
</Steps>

**Practical considerations:**

| Operation | Safe? | How to Make Safe |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| Add column (nullable) | Yes | Always safe. NULL default, no locks on modern DBs. |
| Add column (NOT NULL, no default) | **No** | Add as nullable first, backfill, then add NOT NULL constraint |
| Remove column | **No** | Only after all application versions stop reading it |
| Rename column | **No** | Add new column, migrate data, update app, drop old column |
| Add index | Mostly | Use `CREATE INDEX CONCURRENTLY` (PostgreSQL) to avoid table locks |
| Change column type | **No** | Add new column with new type, migrate, update app, drop old |

**CI/CD integration:**
- Migrations run as a pre-deployment step (Kubernetes init container, or Flyway/Liquibase in CI)
- Migration health check: after migration, verify the schema matches expected state
- Rollback plan: migrations should be reversible. For each `up` migration, write a corresponding `down` migration
- **Never** run destructive migrations (DROP COLUMN, DROP TABLE) automatically in CI. These should require manual approval.

<Warning>
**Large table migrations can lock the table for minutes.** Adding a column to a 500M row table in MySQL can lock writes for 5+ minutes. PostgreSQL handles this better (metadata-only for nullable columns) but backfills are still I/O-heavy. For large tables: add the column in one migration, backfill in batches in a separate job, add the constraint in a final migration. Never combine all three in one lock.
</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Interview: Compare GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, and Jenkins for a team of 100 engineers">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

| Aspect | GitHub Actions | GitLab CI | Jenkins |
|--------|---------------|-----------|---------|
| **Hosting** | GitHub-hosted (or self-hosted runners) | GitLab-hosted (or self-hosted runners) | Self-hosted only |
| **Config** | YAML (`.github/workflows/`) | YAML (`.gitlab-ci.yml`) | Groovy (Jenkinsfile) |
| **Marketplace** | 20,000+ community actions | Templates + includes | 1,800+ plugins |
| **Self-hosted runners** | Easy setup, auto-scaling with ARC | Easy setup, auto-scaling native | You manage everything |
| **Security** | OIDC, environments, branch protection | Built-in SAST/DAST, container scanning | Plugin-dependent |
| **Monorepo support** | Path filters, reusable workflows | Rules with `changes:` keyword | Multibranch with scripted logic |
| **Caching** | Action-based (limited to 10GB per repo) | Built-in cache with CDN | Custom (usually NFS or S3) |
| **Cost** | Free tier + per-minute billing for hosted runners | Free tier + per-minute for hosted | Free (OSS) but infrastructure cost |
| **Learning curve** | Low | Low-Medium | High (Groovy DSL, plugin management) |
| **Best for** | GitHub-native teams, most modern teams | GitLab-native, security-focused organizations | Legacy, extreme customization needs |

**My recommendation for 100 engineers:**

- **If on GitHub:** GitHub Actions with self-hosted runners (using Actions Runner Controller on Kubernetes for auto-scaling). Reusable workflows for shared CI logic across repos.
- **If on GitLab:** GitLab CI is excellent and integrated. Use GitLab runners on Kubernetes with auto-scaling.
- **Jenkins:** Only if you already have it, it works, and migration cost exceeds maintenance cost. For new projects, there is no reason to choose Jenkins in 2025.

**The monorepo challenge at 100 engineers:** The key is affected-based CI. With 100 engineers, you might have 50 PRs/day. Building everything on every PR wastes thousands of compute-minutes. Use path filters (GitHub Actions: `paths:`, GitLab: `rules.changes:`) to only build/test affected services. Pair with a build system that understands the dependency graph (Nx, Turborepo, Bazel).
</Accordion>

## 15. Platform Engineering War Stories

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="How a Helm Values Typo Deleted a Production Database">

A platform engineer at a mid-size SaaS company was deploying a routine update to their PostgreSQL Helm chart. The Helm values file had a `persistence.enabled: true` flag. During a refactor of the values structure, the flag was accidentally nested under the wrong key. Helm rendered the template with `persistence.enabled` defaulting to `false`. The new StatefulSet was deployed without a persistent volume claim.

PostgreSQL started with an ephemeral `emptyDir` volume. It initialized a fresh database (empty). The application connected to this fresh database and started serving 404s for every API call. By the time the on-call engineer noticed, the old PersistentVolumeClaim had been garbage-collected by the StatefulSet controller (because the new StatefulSet did not reference it).

**Recovery:** The PV's reclaim policy was set to `Delete` (the default). The underlying EBS volume was already deleted. Recovery came from the most recent RDS snapshot (they had a backup, fortunately). Data loss: 47 minutes.

**Lessons:**
1. **Never set PV reclaim policy to Delete for databases.** Use `Retain`. Even if the PVC is deleted, the underlying volume persists for manual recovery.
2. **Helm diff before apply.** Use `helm diff upgrade` (helm-diff plugin) to see exactly what will change before applying. The diff would have shown the PVC being removed.
3. **OPA/Kyverno policy:** Enforce that StatefulSets for database workloads must have persistence enabled. Reject any manifest that creates a database StatefulSet without a PVC.
4. **Backups tested regularly.** They had backups but had never tested restore speed. The 47-minute recovery was 47 minutes of "will this actually work?"

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="How Netflix's Chaos Engineering Prevented a Multi-Region Outage">

In 2014, Netflix ran an experiment with Chaos Kong -- a tool that simulates the failure of an entire AWS region. During one such test, they discovered that their user profile service had an undocumented dependency on a regional Redis cluster. When the simulated us-east-1 failure occurred, the profile service in us-west-2 tried to connect to the us-east-1 Redis cluster for session data, timed out, and started failing. Without chaos testing, this dependency would have been invisible until a real regional failure.

The fix was straightforward: replicate the Redis cluster cross-region and update the service to use the local cluster. But the discovery was only possible because they proactively injected failures at the regional level -- something no unit test or integration test would ever catch.

**The chaos engineering principle:** You do not know your system's failure modes until you test them in production. The blast radius of a controlled chaos experiment is always smaller than the blast radius of discovering the same failure during a real incident.

Netflix's chaos engineering stack has since evolved into a suite of tools: Chaos Monkey (random instance termination), Chaos Kong (regional failure), Chaos Gorilla (AZ failure), and FIT (Failure Injection Testing for specific dependency failures). Each tool tests a different failure mode, and together they provide confidence that Netflix can survive infrastructure failures without user-visible impact.

</Accordion>
<Accordion title="The Day Kubernetes Auto-Scaling Bankrupted a Startup's Cloud Budget">

A Y Combinator startup configured their HPA (Horizontal Pod Autoscaler) to scale based on CPU utilization with a target of 50%. Their API service normally ran 3 replicas. One morning, a partner integrated with their API and sent 100x the expected traffic for a data migration.

The HPA worked as designed: it scaled from 3 to 30 replicas in minutes. Cluster Autoscaler launched new nodes to accommodate. The problem: each node was an `m5.2xlarge` (8 vCPU, 32GB RAM, $0.384/hour) because the node group was configured with that single instance type. 30 replicas needed 10 new nodes. The traffic spike lasted 6 hours.

Cost of the spike: ~$23 (10 nodes x 6 hours x $0.384/hour). That is actually fine. The real cost came from what happened next: **the scale-down was broken.** The Cluster Autoscaler had a `scale-down-delay-after-add` of 10 minutes (default) but `scale-down-unneeded-time` was set to 30 minutes, and several nodes had system pods (kube-dns, monitoring) that prevented scale-down entirely. The cluster stayed at 13 nodes for 3 weeks before anyone noticed. Cost: ~$3,000 in unnecessary compute.

**Fixes:**
1. Use **Karpenter** instead of Cluster Autoscaler -- Karpenter is more aggressive about deprovisioning nodes and can consolidate workloads onto fewer, right-sized nodes.
2. Set **HPA `maxReplicas`** to a sane limit (e.g., 20). Infinite scaling is never correct.
3. **Cost alerts:** Set up daily spending alerts at 150% of baseline. A $50/day cluster costing $75/day should trigger investigation.
4. **Mixed instance types:** Use spot instances for stateless workloads and multiple instance families. Karpenter does this automatically.

</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Interview: How do you measure and improve developer productivity on a platform team?">

**Strong Answer Framework:**

**What NOT to do:** Do not measure lines of code, number of commits, or PRs per developer. These vanity metrics incentivize gaming and measure activity, not productivity.

**What to measure:**

1. **DORA metrics** at the team level (not individual): deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR. These are output metrics -- they measure how effectively the team delivers software.

2. **Developer experience metrics** (leading indicators):
  - Time from "I want to create a new service" to "it is deployed in staging with CI/CD, monitoring, and alerting" → target: &lt; 30 minutes
  - Time from "I pushed a commit" to "CI passes and I can merge" → target: &lt; 10 minutes
   - Number of tickets filed to the platform team per month → trend should be decreasing (more self-service)

3. **Qualitative feedback:** Quarterly developer experience survey with questions like:
   - "How easy is it to deploy a change to production?" (1-5)
   - "When something breaks, how easy is it to find the root cause?" (1-5)
   - "What is your biggest productivity bottleneck?"
   - The free-text answers are often more valuable than the scores.

4. **Toil measurement:** Track time spent on operational work (incident response, manual deploys, infrastructure requests) vs. engineering work (building features, improving systems). If >30% of a team's time is operational, the platform has gaps.

**How to improve based on data:**
- If deployment frequency is low → investigate: is CI slow? Is the deployment process manual? Are engineers afraid to deploy?
- If lead time is high → is code review slow (process problem) or are PRs too large (cultural problem)?
- If change failure rate is high → improve testing (more integration tests, canary deployments)
- If developer survey scores are low on "finding root cause" → invest in observability (tracing, log correlation, dashboards)

**The key insight:** Platform teams exist to maximize the *leverage* of every other engineer. Every hour a platform engineer invests in self-service tooling saves 10-100 hours across the organization. Measure your success by how much faster everyone else ships, not by how many Kubernetes clusters you manage.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 17. Container Security Deep Dive

### 17.1 Container Image Security

| Practice | Why | Implementation |
|----------|-----|---------------|
| **Use minimal base images** | Fewer packages = fewer vulnerabilities | Distroless (Google), Alpine, or scratch for Go binaries |
| **Scan images in CI** | Catch vulnerabilities before deployment | Trivy, Grype, or Snyk in the CI pipeline. Block on critical/high CVEs. |
| **Pin image digests** | Tag `latest` is mutable and unpredictable | Use `image: nginx@sha256:abc123...` instead of `image: nginx:latest` |
| **Multi-stage builds** | Build tools (compilers, devDeps) not in final image | `FROM node:20 AS builder` → `FROM node:20-slim` for runtime |
| **Non-root user** | Limit container process privileges | `USER 1000:1000` in Dockerfile. Kubernetes: `runAsNonRoot: true` |
| **Read-only filesystem** | Prevent runtime modification | `securityContext: readOnlyRootFilesystem: true` |
| **Sign images** | Verify image authenticity and integrity | Cosign (Sigstore) to sign, admission controller to verify |

### 17.2 Pod Security Standards

Kubernetes Pod Security Standards (replacing PodSecurityPolicies) define three levels:

| Level | Description | Key Restrictions |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Privileged** | No restrictions | For system components (CNI, storage drivers) only |
| **Baseline** | Prevents known privilege escalations | No hostNetwork, no hostPID, no privileged containers |
| **Restricted** | Hardened security | Must run as non-root, read-only fs, drop all capabilities, seccomp profile |

<Note>
**The default for all application workloads should be Restricted.** Only system-level components (monitoring agents, networking plugins, storage drivers) should use Baseline or Privileged. Enforce this with namespace labels:
```yaml
apiVersion: v1
kind: Namespace
metadata:
  labels:
    pod-security.kubernetes.io/enforce: restricted
```
</Note>
