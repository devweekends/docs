# Distributed Operating Systems: The Architecture of Scale

A Distributed Operating System (DOS) is the evolution of the OS into the realm of the network. It aims to manage a collection of independent, networked, and communicating nodes as a single, coherent computing resource. Think of it like the difference between a solo musician and an orchestra -- a solo musician just plays, but an orchestra needs a conductor, shared sheet music, timing synchronization, and a plan for when someone misses a note.

While pure DOS (like Amoeba or Plan 9) are rare today, the principles they pioneered underpin every cloud platform and large-scale web application. If you have used Kubernetes, DynamoDB, or Kafka, you have used systems built on these foundations.

This module provides a senior-level exploration of distributed coordination, consensus, and the mathematical limits of networked systems.

## 1. The Mathematical Boundaries

In a distributed system, we lose the luxury of a "Global Truth" (no shared clock, no shared memory). This is the fundamental difference from a single-machine OS: on one machine, you can always ask "what time is it?" and get a single answer. Across machines connected by a network, you cannot -- and this single fact cascades into every design decision.

### 1.1. The FLP Impossibility
The **Fisher, Lynch, Paterson (1985)** theorem is the most important result in distributed computing. It is to distributed systems what the halting problem is to computation -- a hard mathematical limit on what is achievable.

- **The Result**: In an asynchronous network where even **one** process can fail by stopping, it is impossible to reach **Consensus** (agreement) with 100% certainty.
- **Why?**: You cannot distinguish between a process that has crashed and a process that is just very slow. Imagine you are on a phone call and the other person goes silent -- have they hung up, or are they just thinking? You can never be sure, and this uncertainty makes guaranteed agreement impossible.
- **The Workaround**: Real systems use **Timeouts** or **Randomization** to bypass FLP. Raft uses timeouts (if I do not hear from the leader in X milliseconds, I start an election). This is technically not "guaranteed" consensus -- but in practice, it works with extremely high probability.

**A senior engineer would say**: "FLP tells us consensus is impossible in theory, but Raft and Paxos work in practice because they trade mathematical certainty for practical reliability using timeouts and leader election."

### 1.2. The CAP Theorem Revisited
Every distributed data store must choose two out of three:
1.  **Consistency**: All nodes see the same data at the same time.
2.  **Availability**: Every request gets a response (success or failure).
3.  **Partition Tolerance**: The system works despite network drops.

**The Reality**: You *must* choose Partition Tolerance ($P$) because network partitions are not a choice -- they happen in every real datacenter. So the real choice is between $CP$ (consistency over availability: PostgreSQL, Zookeeper, etcd) and $AP$ (availability over consistency: Cassandra, DynamoDB in default mode).

**Practical tip**: CAP is a useful mental model but oversimplified. In practice, most systems are not strictly CP or AP -- they offer tunable consistency. DynamoDB lets you choose strong consistency per-read. Cassandra lets you set consistency levels per-query. The real question is: "What consistency does this specific operation need?" not "Is the whole system CP or AP?"

## 2. Time, Ordering, and Causality

Without a shared clock, we cannot say "Event A happened at 10:00:01 and B at 10:00:02." Wall clocks drift (even NTP-synced clocks can be off by milliseconds), so we need a different notion of "order." The solution is **Logical Clocks** -- they do not tell you *when* something happened, but they tell you *whether one thing happened before another*, which is often all you need.

### 2.1. Lamport Clocks ($L$)
A simple integer that increments on every local event and is attached to every message.
- **Rule**: $L(receive) = \max(L_{local}, L_{incoming}) + 1$.
- **Limitation**: If $L(A) < L(B)$, we don't know if $A \to B$. They might be independent.

### 2.2. Vector Clocks ($V$)
To capture **Concurrence**, each node keeps a vector of clocks for *all* nodes.
- **Conflict Detection**: If $V_A$ has a higher value for Node 1, but $V_B$ has a higher value for Node 2, the events are **Concurrent** (a write conflict).

### 2.3. Google Spanner and TrueTime
Spanner uses specialized hardware (GPS + Atomic Clocks) to provide a "Confidence Interval" $[\epsilon_{low}, \epsilon_{high}]$ for time.
- **Commit Wait**: The system waits until the current time is guaranteed to be past the commit time of the previous transaction.
- **Result**: Global linearizability (Strict Consistency) at scale.

## 3. Consensus: The Heart of Coordination

Consensus is the process of getting $N$ nodes to agree on a single value (e.g., "Who is the leader?").

### 3.1. Paxos (The Academic Foundation)
Designed by Leslie Lamport. It is notoriously difficult to understand but mathematically robust.
- **Phases**: Prepare (Ask for permission), Propose (Send value), Accept (Value committed).
- **Quorums**: You only need agreement from a majority ($N/2 + 1$) to proceed.

### 3.2. Raft (The Practical Standard)
Designed to be "understandable."
1.  **Leader Election**: One node is elected leader.
2.  **Log Replication**: Clients send writes to the leader; the leader replicates them to followers.
3.  **Safety**: A follower only votes for a candidate whose log is at least as up-to-date as its own.

## 4. Naming and Discovery: Consistent Hashing

How do you find which of 10,000 servers holds the key `"user:123"`?

### 4.1. The Hash Ring
1.  Hash all server IDs into a $2^{160}$ (SHA-1) space (a circle).
2.  Hash the key into the same space.
3.  The key belongs to the **first server encountered clockwise**.
- **The Benefit**: When a server joins or leaves, you only move $1/N$ of the keys. Standard modulo hashing ($Hash(key) \% N$) would require moving *every* key.

## 5. Distributed Transactions: 2PC vs. Sagas

### 5.1. Two-Phase Commit (2PC)
Think of 2PC like a wedding ceremony: the officiant (coordinator) asks each person "Do you?" (Prepare phase). Only if both say "I do" does the officiant say "I now pronounce you..." (Commit phase).

1.  **Prepare**: Coordinator asks all participants "Can you commit?" Each participant writes a prepare record to its WAL and locks resources.
2.  **Commit**: If all say "Yes," the coordinator writes a commit record and sends the "Commit" command to all participants.
- **The Flaw**: It is a **Blocking Protocol**. If the coordinator dies after sending "Prepare" but before sending "Commit," all participants are stuck holding locks forever, waiting for a decision that may never come. This is why 2PC is rarely used across microservices -- it couples availability to the coordinator's uptime.

### 5.2. Sagas (The Modern Microservices Way)
Instead of one big transaction, break it into small, independent transactions that each have a **compensating action** (an undo operation). Think of it like booking a vacation: you book the flight, then the hotel, then the car. If the car booking fails, you cancel the hotel and refund the flight -- each step is independently reversible.

- **Compensating Actions**: If Step 3 fails, the system executes "Undo" actions for Steps 1 and 2 (e.g., "Refund payment" instead of "Rollback database"). Unlike 2PC, no global lock is held -- each step commits independently.
- **Tradeoff**: Sagas provide **eventual consistency**, not strict atomicity. There is a window where Step 1 is committed but Step 2 has not yet started. Your system must tolerate this intermediate state.

## 6. Resource Scheduling: Borg and Mesos

A Distributed OS must schedule tasks across the cluster.

### 6.1. Two-Level Scheduling (Mesos)
- **Level 1**: Mesos offers raw resources (CPU/RAM) to frameworks (Spark, Kubernetes).
- **Level 2**: The framework decides which specific tasks to run on those resources.

### 6.2. Dominant Resource Fairness (DRF)
In a cluster with CPU and RAM, how do you divide fairly?
- **Logic**: A user's "share" is defined by their **Dominant Resource** (the one they use the most of). DRF ensures everyone's dominant share is equalized.

## 7. Senior Interview Deep Dive

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Q1: Why do we use Quorums ($N/2+1$) instead of requiring all nodes to agree?">
    If we required all nodes to agree, the system would stop working if even **one** node crashed (Zero Availability). 
    
    By using a Majority Quorum, the system can tolerate up to $f$ failures in a system of $2f+1$ nodes. This provides a balance between Consistency (two majorities must overlap by at least one node) and Availability.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Q2: Explain the 'Split Brain' problem and how to prevent it.">
    Split Brain occurs when a network partition divides a cluster into two halves, and both halves think the other half is dead. Both might then elect a leader and start accepting conflicting writes.
    
    **Prevention**: Use **Quorums**. Only the half that has a majority of the nodes is allowed to elect a leader and proceed. The smaller half must stop accepting writes.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Q3: What is 'Gossip' and why is it used for failure detection?">
    Gossip (Epidemic) protocols involve nodes periodically sending their state to a random subset of other nodes. 
    
    **Advantage**: It is extremely scalable ($O(\log N)$ convergence) and robust. There is no single point of failure (like a master monitor). If a node stops gossiping, its neighbors will eventually mark it as dead and spread the word.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## 8. Practice: Building a Distributed Lock

**Challenge**: Use **etcd** or **Redis** (with Redlock) to implement a distributed lock that handles process crashes and network partitions.

```python
# Conceptual pseudocode using etcd (CP system).
# The key insight: the lock is a key in etcd with a lease (TTL).
# If the holder crashes, the lease expires and the lock auto-releases.
def acquire_lock(lock_name, lease_ttl=10):
    # Create a lease -- etcd will delete the key if we don't renew within TTL.
    # This prevents the "holder died while holding the lock" problem.
    lease = etcd.lease(ttl=lease_ttl)

    # Atomic put-if-absent -- etcd's linearizable guarantee ensures
    # only ONE client can succeed, even under concurrent attempts.
    success = etcd.put(lock_name, "active", lease=lease, if_not_exists=True)

    if success:
        # Background thread sends keep-alive RPCs to extend the lease.
        # If our process hangs or the network partitions, the lease
        # expires and another client can acquire the lock.
        lease.keep_alive()
        return True
    return False
```

**Practical tip**: Distributed locks are subtly broken in most naive implementations. The key pitfall: your process acquires the lock, does work, but the lease expires (due to a GC pause or network delay) before the work finishes. Now another process acquires the same lock and both are operating concurrently. The fix is **fencing tokens**: etcd returns a monotonically increasing revision with each lock acquisition, and downstream resources reject operations with stale revision numbers.

## Interview Deep-Dive

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="A candidate says 'CAP theorem means you can only have two of three.' Push back on this. What is the real nuance?">
    **Strong Answer:**

    - The "pick two of three" framing is misleading because it implies a static, system-wide choice. CAP applies per-operation during a partition event. When the network is healthy, you can have all three.
    - The real constraint: you MUST tolerate partitions (P) because networks are unreliable. The meaningful choice is what happens DURING a partition: reject writes to maintain consistency (CP), or accept writes on both sides and reconcile later (AP).
    - Real systems make different choices for different operations. DynamoDB is AP for regular reads (eventual consistency) but offers strongly consistent reads (CP behavior). PostgreSQL with synchronous replication is CP; with async replication, closer to AP with potential data loss.
    - PACELC is more useful: during a Partition choose Availability or Consistency; Else choose Latency or Consistency. Cassandra is PA/EL. Spanner is PC/EC.
    - Practical takeaway: define consistency per-operation (reads can be eventually consistent, financial writes must be linearizable) and choose your data store accordingly.

    **Follow-up: How does Spanner achieve global strong consistency?**

    Spanner uses TrueTime (GPS + atomic clocks providing bounded time uncertainty, typically under 7ms). On commit, it performs a "commit wait" until the current time is guaranteed past the commit timestamp. This ensures linearizability. During a majority partition, Paxos groups cannot reach quorum and writes are rejected (it chooses consistency). But Google's network is so reliable that partitions are extremely rare.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Explain the difference between Lamport clocks and vector clocks. When would Lamport clocks give a wrong answer?">
    **Strong Answer:**

    - Lamport clocks assign a single integer to events. Rule: before sending, increment; on receive, set to max(local, received) + 1. If A causally precedes B, then L(A) is less than L(B). But the converse does not hold -- L(A) less than L(B) does NOT mean A happened before B. They might be concurrent.
    - Vector clocks fix this. Each node maintains a vector of counters. To compare: if V(A) is element-wise less-than-or-equal to V(B), A happened-before B. If neither dominates, the events are concurrent.
    - The failure scenario: Client 1 writes key=X with L=5, Client 2 writes key=Y with L=7 on different nodes (no causal relationship). With Lamport clocks, you might incorrectly impose an ordering. If both then write to the same key, you need to detect the conflict (concurrent writes) for proper conflict resolution. Lamport clocks cannot detect this; vector clocks can.
    - Trade-off: vector clocks scale linearly with node count. Dynamo-style systems use "dotted version vectors" or bound the vector size.

    **Follow-up: How does Raft handle a leader failing mid-replication?**

    Followers notice missing heartbeats and start an election. A candidate only gets votes from followers whose log is not more up-to-date. This ensures the new leader has all committed entries. Uncommitted entries from the old leader that were only replicated to a minority may be lost. Raft requires majority acknowledgment before telling the client "success" -- so committed entries are always safe.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Two-Phase Commit is called a blocking protocol. What exactly blocks, and what are the alternatives?">
    **Strong Answer:**

    - In 2PC, if the coordinator crashes after Prepare but before Commit/Abort, participants that voted Yes are stuck. They hold locks, have done tentative work, and cannot commit (maybe another participant voted No) or abort (maybe the coordinator was about to commit). They wait for coordinator recovery, during which locked resources are unavailable.
    - I have seen a production incident where a 2PC coordinator crashed during a cross-database transaction. Both databases had locked rows for 15 minutes until manual recovery.
    - Alternatives: (1) Sagas -- break into independent local transactions with compensating actions. Never blocks, but no atomicity (intermediate states are visible). (2) Three-Phase Commit -- adds a pre-commit phase allowing unilateral decisions on coordinator failure, but requires a reliable failure detector (impossible per FLP). (3) Consensus-based commit (Spanner uses Paxos per shard) -- replaces the single coordinator with a replicated state machine, so coordinator failure does not block.

    **Follow-up: In a saga, how do you handle a compensating action that itself fails?**

    Production systems handle this with: (1) retries with exponential backoff (compensations must be idempotent), (2) dead letter queues for exhausted retries requiring manual intervention, (3) reconciliation jobs that periodically scan for and fix incomplete sagas. This is why sagas require careful design of idempotent compensations and monitoring for stuck sagas.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
